In contemporary digital ecosystems, news and their repercussions propagate almost instantaneously. Our research aims to characterize the spatio-temporal patterns that emerge from these information flows. To this end, we analyze large-scale datasets from social media and news outlets, and develop mathematical frameworks that explain collective behavior based on elementary interactions among individuals.

Our theoretical work draws on statistical mechanics, nonlinear dynamics, and complex network theory, while our empirical methodology incorporates a broad suite of machine learning algorithms for data processing and analysis.

    • Research

    Opinion formation

    The dynamics of opinion formation within groups and across social networks have attracted considerable attention in recent years. Emerging collective patterns often arise in nontrivial ways due to the inherent complexity of individuals and, crucially, their interactions. To investigate these phenomena, we employ computational models incorporating diverse behavioral mechanisms to uncover the conditions under which such emergent dynamics arise. We complement these simulations with analytical developments aimed at deriving equations that characterize the underlying processes. Additionally, we work with data-driven, multidimensional agent-based models to study how, in polarized contexts, individuals tend to adopt increasingly extreme and correlated opinions.

    Information Diffusion in Social Networks

    The dissemination of information through social networks has become a central feature of contemporary communication, as an increasing number of individuals rely on these platforms as their primary source of news. The pathways through which information circulates—shaped by algorithms, user interactions, and network structure—play a crucial role in shaping public opinion on a wide range of issues. Understanding how content propagates, how users are exposed to it, and how visibility patterns emerge is therefore essential for characterizing the broader socio-informational landscape. Our research examines these diffusion processes by analyzing large-scale datasets and developing theoretical and computational frameworks that allow us to identify the mechanisms governing reach, amplification, and the formation of informational echo chambers.

    Information Dinamics

    In this line of research, we analyze and model the dynamics of information propagation across multiple scales, ranging from the temporal evolution of topics and their geographic distribution to the identification of finer informational units such as textual quotes or memes. By examining how these elements emerge, transform, and spread across different media ecosystems, we aim to uncover the mechanisms that structure the flow of information in complex environments. Our approach combines large-scale data analysis with mathematical and computational modeling, allowing us to characterize patterns of amplification, decay, and recombination that shape the informational landscape. Through this framework, we seek to better understand how narratives evolve, how specific fragments gain prominence, and how these processes ultimately influence collective attention and public discourse.

    Language Evolution

    In this research area, we seek to identify the sociocultural and cognitive components that shape the frequency and evolution of word usage, and to model the regular patterns that unfold over centuries as expressions of collective cognition. By analyzing large corpora spanning long historical periods, we use statistical and computational tools to detect systematic trends, lexical innovations, and shifts in semantic fields. These methods allow us to characterize how linguistic structures emerge, stabilize, or fade, and to quantify the influence of social dynamics, technological change, and cultural transmission on the evolution of language. Through this framework, we aim to understand not only the temporal trajectories of individual words, but also the broader mechanisms that govern linguistic adaptation in complex societies.

    Decision-making in sports

    In this line of research, we investigate decision-making processes in competitive sports through an integrated approach that combines data collection, statistical analysis, and computational modeling. We study how athletes and teams make strategic choices under conditions of uncertainty, time pressure, and interaction with opponents. Our methodology draws on network science to characterize passing structures and spatial organization in football, statistical and cognitive models to analyze decision patterns in chess, and stochastic process frameworks to examine the dynamics of rallies and point-to-point actions in sports such as volleyball and padel. Through these tools, we aim to identify the principles that govern individual and collective decision-making, and to understand how they influence performance and competitive outcomes.
